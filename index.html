Core wisdom from two masters

"The only way the individual is going to end up with a reasonable outcome is to educate themselves."
— David Swensen, Yale CIO
1
Costs are the enemyEvery fee comes directly out of your return. A 1% annual fee on $500k costs over $100k across 20 years. Bogle called this "the tyranny of compounding costs."
2
Own the market, don't beat itActive managers charge more and almost always underperform broad index funds after fees. Own the whole market at the lowest possible cost.
3
Diversify across asset classesSwensen recommended spreading across domestic equities, foreign equities, real estate, bonds, and inflation-protected bonds — not just U.S. stocks and bonds.
4
Stay the courseMarkets fall. The investor who panics sells low and buys high. Conviction requires understanding — which is why reading matters.
5
Rebalance regularlyMarkets drift your allocation away from your targets. Rebalancing forces you to sell high and buy low — the opposite of what most investors do instinctively.
6
Tax sensitivity mattersSwensen found taxes can reduce equity returns by 3+ percentage points per year. Use tax-deferred accounts first; hold bonds inside them.

The Clash of the Cultures (2012)

"When enterprise becomes the bubble on a whirlpool of speculation … the job of capitalism is likely to be ill-done."
— John Maynard Keynes, quoted by Bogle
Bogle's ten rules for investors
1
Remember reversion to the meanYesterday's top fund is rarely tomorrow's. Past performance is not prologue — it is almost always a trap. Hot funds cool; winning strategies get crowded out.
2
Time is your friend, impulse is your enemyCompound interest works slowly. Panic sells and euphoric buys destroy portfolios. Market timing requires two correct decisions in a row — nearly impossible.
3
Buy right and hold tightGet your stock/bond balance right for your situation, then hold it. Only change it as your life changes — not as the market changes.
4
Have realistic expectationsThe "bagel" is real investment return — dividends plus earnings growth. The "doughnut" is speculative return from changing valuations. Eat the bagel; don't count on the doughnut.
5
Forget the needle, buy the haystackDon't try to pick winning stocks. Buy the whole market through a broad index fund and capture everything it produces.
6
Minimize the croupier's takeWall Street is a casino — the house always wins its cut. Every fee, commission, and expense ratio is a guaranteed loss before you even begin.
7
There is no escaping riskStocks are volatile. Bonds lose to inflation. Cash loses to both. The goal is not to eliminate risk but to be compensated fairly for the risk you take.
8
Beware of fighting the last warInvestors pile into what just worked. Gold after it soared. Tech after the bubble. The strategy that dominated last decade rarely repeats.
9
The hedgehog bests the foxThe fox knows many things; the hedgehog knows one big thing. Know one thing: own the market, keep costs low, stay the course.
10
Stay the courseBogle's last word, always. The investor who holds through crashes, bubbles, and uncertainty almost always beats the investor who reacts to them.
"We investors don't get what we pay for. We get precisely what we don't pay for. So, the less we pay, the more we get."
— John C. Bogle, The Clash of the Cultures

Lessons the market has already taught

1929 – 1932
The Great Crash
-90% small stocks
Small stocks fell 90% peak to trough. But $1 invested in June 1932 grew 100,000-fold by 2003. Swensen's lesson: diversification helps investors survive concentrated collapse and stay in the game for the recovery.
1966 – 1974
The Go-Go Years and their aftermath
Speculation, then ruin
Bogle lived this personally. Hot funds chasing momentum peaked and crashed. Wellington Fund, which he ran, lost most of its assets. He called it his greatest mistake — and the restoration of patient, balanced investing his greatest recovery.
1999 – 2003
Dot-com bust and reversion to the mean
-51% CGM Focus after peak
Bogle documented fund after fund that soared — then collapsed. The CGM Focus Fund returned 917% from 1998–2007, then lost 51% while the S&P fell only 6%. The typical shareholder lost 11% annually chasing it. RTM is relentless.
2008 – 2009
Financial crisis
-57% S&P 500 peak
Investors who panicked and sold locked in their losses. Those who rebalanced — buying stocks when they were cheapest — recovered and outperformed. Speculation in mortgage-backed securities brought the system to its knees. Bogle had warned exactly this would happen.
1926 – 2003
The long-run case for equities
$1 → $2,285 in stocks vs $61 in bonds
Ibbotson data: large U.S. stocks compounded at 10.4% annually. One dollar became $2,285. Bonds became $61. Cash became $18. Both Bogle and Swensen agree: equity ownership, patiently held and broadly diversified, is the engine of long-term wealth.
